Lesson 35

Hallowing the Lord’s Day

Lesson Starts

Text: Exodus 20:8-11; 31:12-16

Memory Verse: "Remember the Sabbath day by keeping it holy. 9 Six days you shall labor and do all your work, 10 but the seventh day is a Sabbath to the Lord your God. On it you shall not do any work, neither you, nor your son or daughter, nor your manservant or maidservant, nor your animals, nor the alien within your gates.” - Exodus 20:8-10

Bottom Line: Worship is more than just observing a day as sacred.

Lesson Objective : This lesson will help the teens to acknowledge Christ as the centre of their worship.

Lesson Introduction

Why do Christians worship on Sunday? Why do we have some other church denominations who worship on Saturday? Who is right and who is wrong? What happens to Christians in countries where Sunday is not a public holiday— when do they gather for worship? Would they be violating any scriptural principle if they worship on any other day apart from Sunday? Have you ever thought along these lines? What kind of answers or ideas are flickering through your mind? Let’s find out if your thoughts kind find any congruence with today’s lesson.

Lesson Insight/Biblical Background

The Sabbath is the practice of observing one day in seven, usually the seventh day of the week, as a time for rest and worship. This practice apparently originated in creation, because God created the universe in six days and rested on the seventh (Gen 2: 2-3). It was based on this understanding that Moses instituted the observance of the Sabbath, as the fourth commandment, when he presented the Ten Commandments to the children of Israel (vv.8-11). In keeping the Sabbath holy, Israelites were to abstain from all forms of work. Anyone who flouted this law risked the penalty of death (Ex. 31:15). Next to the ritual of circumcision, the observance of the Sabbath became the distinctive mark of the Jewish race up until the first century A.D during the advent of Christ and even beyond it.

Lesson Commentary

I. The Significance Of The Sabbath

A few significant points need to be clarified in view of the Mosaic institution of the Sabbath. This may help us in appreciating, what the Sabbath signifies for the Jews and how we may as Christians relate to it today.

A. Importantly, it should be noted that the fourth commandment wasn’t given in isolation, but it was given in relationship to the preceding three, those which bear upon Israel’s relationship to God, forbidding the worship of other gods and the use of idols (Ex 20:1-5). The commandment makes a significant contribution to the Israel’s (true) worship of God. God was teaching His people more about worship, than about observance of a particular day.

B. The observance of the Sabbath was declared to be the sign of the Mosaic Covenant between God and His chosen people Israel (30:13). It was also a reminder of how God delivered them from slavery in Egypt (Deut. 5:12-15). Their redemption and exodus was a kind of Sabbath from the toil of bondage. God’s covenant with His people and their redemption was key to the Sabbath.

C. Since Sabbath observance was the sign of the covenant, observing the Sabbath was the Israelites’ pledge to keep the whole Law. To break this commandment was to reject all of the Law. This made obedience to the law very important, even more important with the death penalty prescribed for its violation. Obedience was a significant element of the Sabbath.

D. The commandment wasn’t just a requirement to “keep the Sabbath,” but more of an instruction to “keep the Sabbath holy” (20:8). Keeping the Sabbath involves much more than abstinence from labour, it requires the acknowledgment of the sacredness, the sanctity, of this day because of God’s deeds and declaration. The words and works of a Holy God was supposed to be the focus of the people in keeping the Sabbath, not just the day.

E. The purpose of Israel’s Sabbath observance was to teach them about sanctification (31:13). Just as God had set the seventh day apart from the other six at creation, so He had set Israel apart by His divine calling and their deliverance from slavery. In keeping the Sabbath holy, the Jews were then to keep themselves holy (set apart). The keeping of the Sabbath thus became an essential lesson in sanctification.

II. The Sabbath and The Lord’s Day

Early Christians chose to adopt the first day of the week, Sunday, for worship, undoubtedly because Christ rose on that day. Designated as the Lord’s Day, Sunday became a day of worship and the celebration of the Resurrection and fellowship with one another and with the risen Christ whom Christians trust as Saviour and worship as God. Although some Christian denominations still observe the Sabbath (Saturday) as a day of worship, the Christian community as a whole does not keep the Sabbath. There is often controversies between those who observe Saturday and those who adopt Sunday as the day of worship, just as it was the case, from inception, between early Christians who gather on Sundays and adherents of Judaism, who insist that worship must be on Sabbath, in the synagogues. Christians continue to uphold Sunday as the Lord’s Day, because there are no commandments or exhortations, explicitly or implicitly, in the New Testament in support of observing Sabbath. Rather, the early church gathered on the first day of the week in celebration and remembrance of the resurrection. Christians also insist on not keeping the Sabbath because it was a part of the old covenant, legal requirements of the Law, and according to the New Testament Christians today are not under such legal requirements like circumcision, ceremonial washings, animal sacrifices and the rest. Instead, under the new covenant (2 Cor. 3:6) New Testament believers are to produce the righteousness of the Law, the character of Christ, through the Spirit (Rom. 6:14; 8:1-4; 2 Cor. 3:4-18; Gal. 3-5).

III. The Supremacy of Christ Over Sabbath

Although Jesus observed the Sabbath by attending the synagogue meetings, His teachings and some of His actions, showed there was no need to overemphasise its observance. Once, He told off the Pharisees, whose interpretation of the Sabbath law, had then neutralized its significance. They had accused His disciples of plucking corn, from a corn plantation on a Sabbath. Jesus answered them, “The Sabbath was made for man, and not man for the Sabbath” (Mark 2:27). In Matthew’s rendering of the same incident, Jesus said, “For the Son of Man is Lord of the Sabbath.” Christ being God and the mediator of the New Covenant is supreme over the Sabbath. (Matt 12:8) At another time, when the Pharisees questioned Jesus for healing a man’s withered hand on a Sabbath, He answered them with a question, “Which is lawful on the Sabbath: to do good or to do evil, to save life or to kill?” (Mark 3:4) They couldn’t answer Him. Jesus and His disciples were not guilty of violating any scriptural law governing the Sabbath, but only conflicted with the Pharisees’ interpretation of the law. Rather than uphold the significance of the Sabbath law, they overemphasised its trivialities, making it burdensome for the people to observe. This was what Christ came to change. What was the point hallowing a day, without hallowing the Lord of the day. Christ is the Lord of not just the Sabbath, but the Lord of every other day. Paul, reinforced the supremacy and the sufficiency of Christ over the Sabbath, when he urged the Colossians to "let no one pass judgment on you ... with regard to a festival or a new moon or a Sabbath…” (Col 2:16) This text along with implicit references to the Sabbath in Rom 14:5 and Gal 4:10, and some other texts, underscore New Testament teaching on the observance of the Sabbath or any other day at that.

Discussion Questions/Activities

1. What key themes can you identify from the significance of the Sabbath and what are their implications today?

2. Christ is the Lord of not just the Sabbath, but the Lord of every other day: what is the implication of this statement?

3. In what way is Christ supreme over the Sabbath?

Lesson Summary

Jesus told the Samaritan woman, “Believe me, woman, a time is coming when you will worship the Father neither on this mountain nor in Jerusalem…Yet a time is coming and has now come when the true worshipers will worship the Father in spirit and truth, for they are the kind of worshipers the Father seeks. God is spirit, and his worshipers must worship in spirit and in truth.” (John 4:21-24). The emphasis on worship, shouldn’t be whether it should be on a Sunday or a Saturday; it shouldn’t be whether it should be done in the temple or in the synagogue. True worship demands true worshipers, who are sincere and whose hearts are connected to God. When such people gather to worship, wherever they choose to gather, whichever day they choose to gather, would be hallowed, because God would be present. Are you a true worshiper?
