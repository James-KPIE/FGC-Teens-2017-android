2017 Teens Sunday School Manual

Lesson 31

Peter Preached to Gentiles

Lesson Starts

Text: Acts 10:1-44

Memory Verse: “ While Peter was still speaking these words, the Holy Spirit came on all who heard the message.” - Acts 10:44

Bottom Line: The preaching of the gospel has no boundary.

Lesson Objective: This lesson will challenge the teens to be

prepared to preach to any one as opportunities come.

Lesson Introduction

Jesus came to redeem humanity by paying the wages of our sins through His death on the cross, “For the Son of man is come to seek and to save that which was lost (Luke 19:10). The Gospel of Luke gives an insight into the earthly ministry of Jesus, “…, of all that Jesus began both to do and teach” (Acts 1:1), and what happened after His departure, “…commanded them that they should not depart from Jerusalem, but wait for the promise of the Father...” They needed power of the Holy Spirit to evangelize the world. “For John truly baptized with water; but ye shall be baptized with the Holy Ghost not many days hence… But ye shall receive power, after that the Holy Ghost is come upon you: and ye shall be witnesses unto me both in Jerusalem, and in all Judaea, and in Samaria, and unto the uttermost part of the earth” (Acts 1:2-8). This promise was fulfilled on the day of Pentecost. The Great Commission began on that day. The first Gospel message by Apostle Peter resulted in about three thousand converts. Then God opened the door of salvation to the Gentiles also, “And as I began to speak, the Holy Ghost fell on them, as on us at the beginning” (Acts 11:15). The scripture also declares, “For the promise is unto you, and to your children, and to all that are afar off, even as many as the Lord our God shall call” (Acts 2:39). We are all called to preach the Gospel to the whole world (Matt. 28:18-20, Mark 16:15-16).

Lesson Insight/Biblical Background

The Book of Acts gives a narrative insight into the spread of the Gospel. In Acts chapter two, the Holy Spirit fell on about one hundred and twenty disciples who gathered together in the Upper Room on the ‘Day of Pentecost’. In chapter ten, the gift of the Holy Spirit was also given to the Gentiles who gathered in the house of Cornelius during the preaching of Peter. One important thing to note was the conclusion of Apostle Peter, “Then Peter opened his mouth, and said, of a truth I perceive that God is no respecter of persons: But in every nation he that feareth him, and worketh righteousness, is accepted with him (Acts 10:34-35). The implication is that, the ‘Gospel Message’ is for the whole world (Acts 1:8).

Lesson Outline

I. The Prayer of Cornelius (Acts 10: 1-8)

II. The Vision of Peter (Acts 10: 9-23)

III. The Gentiles Receive The Gift of The

Holy Spirit ( Acts 10: 24-44)

Lesson Commentary

I. The Prayer of Cornelius

Cornelius was in a fasting and praying session in his house when he had a vision of angelic visitation. Cornelius was a centurion, a Roman officer in charge of 100 soldiers, in the Italian Regiment, consisting of 600 soldiers. In the New Testament, centurions are consistently viewed in a favourable light (Matt 8:5-10; Mark 15:44-45; Acts 22:25-26). Centurion Cornelius became one of the first Gentiles after Pentecost to hear the good news of Jesus Christ's forgiveness. Cornelius was described as devout and God-fearing which might have accounted for such divine attention.

II. The Vision of Peter

While Apostle Peter was praying, he fell into a trance in which God gave him a vision of a sheet coming down to earth with all kinds of...animals...reptiles...and birds. When God commanded him to kill and eat these animals, Peter declined. He knew from the Law that he should not eat unclean animals (Lev. 11). His intentions were good, but he was being disobedient. The Jews considered all but themselves unclean. To remove this prejudice, the vision significantly taught that God had cleansed all (Jews and Gentiles) who are faithful to Him.

III. The Gentiles Receive The Gift Of The Holy Spirit.

In bringing salvation to the Gentiles, God had to prepare Peter to bring the message and Cornelius to hear the message. Salvation is a divine work of grace, but God works through human channels. It is interesting to see how religious a person can be and still not be saved. Certainly, Cornelius was sincere in his obedience to God's Law, his fasting, and his generosity to the Jewish people. In every way, he was a model of religious respectability - and yet he was not a saved man. Apostle Peter had to go and preach to him and his people. It is our responsibility to preach the Gospel message to sinners.

Discussion Questions/Activities

1. Apostle Peter said, “Of a truth I perceive that God is no respecter of persons…” What do you understand by this statement?

2. Cornelius and his people received the baptism of the Holy Spirit. What is the significance of Holy Spirit baptism in evangelism?

3. The Gospel message is for the whole world, discuss your own role.

Lesson Summary

1. The statement of Apostle Peter “...of a truth I perceive that God is no respecter of persons: But in every nation he that feareth him, and worketh righteousness, is accepted with him” (Acts 10:34-35), is fundamental and key note in Christianity.

2. The Gospel message of salvation is open to ALL who surrender to the Lordship of Jesus Christ (John 1:12). The scripture declares, “For whosoever shall call upon the name of the Lord shall be saved” (Rom 10:13).

3. The commandment of the Master referred to as The Great Commission demands, “…Go ye into all the world, and preach the gospel to every creature” (Mark 16:15). The Gospel of Matthew says, “Go ye therefore, and teach all nations…” (Matt 28:19).

4. The Holy Spirit does not discriminate as to who should receive the Gospel. We shouldn’t also be discriminatory to those we have tagged “unlikely people”, in our minds— area boys, prostitutes, drug addicts, petty thieves, yahoo boys etc.
