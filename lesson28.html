2017 Teens Sunday School Manual

Lesson 28

Simon is Rebuked

Lesson Starts

Text: Acts 8:9-25

Memory Verse: “Peter answered: "May your money perish with you, because you thought you could buy the gift of God with money!” - Acts 8:20

Bottom Line: The power of God is received through the Holy Ghost after salvation.

Lesson Objective: This lesson will give more understanding to the teens that money or position cannot buy the power of God.

Lesson Introduction

After salvation, the spiritual growth is a process or a journey expressed as in Rom. 12:2 (NIV) “Do not conform any longer to the pattern of this world, but be transformed by the renewing of your mind. Then you will be able to test and approve what God's will is – his good, pleasing and perfect will”. Salvation is a means to an end and not an end in itself. The spiritual life of a believer needs to be nurtured. Simon the sorcerer’s (known as Simon Magus in church tradition) attitude was a display of spiritual childishness; an act of an unregenerate mind who thought the power of God was for sale.

Lesson Insight/Biblical Background

After the death of Stephen, persecution arose against the church and some believers fled Jerusalem. Phillip, a co-labourer with Stephen moved to Samaria and launched out with the gospel of Christ with signs and wonders following his ministry. Phillip’s new work nullified the counterfeit magical arts of Simon the sorcerer that was mesmerizing the Samaritans before the arrival of Phillip. It was a new dawn for the people of Samaria.

Lesson Outline

I. The Confounding of Samaria

II. The Conversion of Samaria

III. The Confinement of Simon

Lesson Commentary

I. The Confounding of Samaria

For a long time, Simon Magus deceived the people of Samaria, earning himself fame and fortune on account of his magical power. Sorcery, which is strongly condemned by God (Deut. 18:9-13), was common in the ancient world. While some acts and demonstrations were no more than illusions of the mind, others were empowered by Satan in an attempt to discredit the power of God (Thess. 2:9). Captured by the spell of Simon’s sorcery, the people of Samaria, both great and small, dignified and even deified him.

II. The Conversion of Samaria

Counterfeit thrives in the absence of the original. With the arrival of Philip from Jerusalem, backed by the power of God, Simon’s befuddlement of Samaritans came to an end. Those who have been enchanted by the sorcerer, received and believed the Gospel and became Christians. Confounded by the ministry of Phillip, Simon himself submitted to the higher power of the gospel of Christ and he admitted to become one of the believers. What is not certain is if Simon genuinely was saved, as the state of his heart became later apparent in the story (vv.18-20). It is not impossible that he may have feigned his conversion to safeguard his popularity with the people, who have now shifted base to Phillip’s ministry.

III. The Confinement Of Simon

News of what was happening in Samaria got to the leaders of the church in Jerusalem. So Peter and John were sent to Samaria to give Philip some support. On arrival, they saw the need to introduce the baptism of the Holy Spirit to the people. This they did by laying on of hands, seeing this, the spiritual blindness and immaturity of Simon Magus was eposed. He thought the power of God was for sale like magic and he offered money in exchange for God’s power. Taken aback and sorely infuriated by Simon’s impious and insolent behaviour, Peter harshly condemned his act and put him in his place. This act of Simon shows conclusively that his motives in becoming associated with the church were not genuine. It was from Simon’s act that the term ‘simony’ was derived, which denotes, the buying and selling of ecclesiastical privileges, appointments or church offices, a practice which became popular when Christianity became the official religion of the Roman empire in the 4th Century A.D.

Discussion Questions/Activities

1. What does Simon’s suspicious conversion tell us about people’s motives in coming to church today?

2. What are the activities of the prototypes of Simon in the church today?

3. How can believers today avoid being victims of those in the likeness of Simon in the church?

Lesson Summary

The power of God is not for sale. That is one big lesson we have learnt today. People come into church for different motives and so they may be in church and have all the forms being a Christian, but their heart may be far from the gospel of Christ. Simon Magus typifies those who Paul described as acting religious, but rejecting the power that could make them godly (2 Tim. 3:5, NLT). Peter’s unreserved rebuke of Simon is an indication that the church should not condone or encourage those who have infiltrated the church to make merchandize of the Gospel. The Christian faith must not permit charlatans to rubbish the gospel of Jesus Christ. We are to contend for the faith that was once for all entrusted to the saints (Jude 3) in faithfully living out the Gospel and in resisting those who make mockery of the Gospel through distortion and deception. Christians must take cognisance of Paul’s warning in 2 Thess. 2:9-10 of the end-time activities of the messengers of Satan displayed in all kinds of counterfeit miracles, signs and wonders, and in every sort of evil that deceives those who are perishing. They perish because they refused to love the truth and so be saved.
