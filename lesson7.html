2017 Teens Sunday School Manual

Lesson 7

The Fruit of the Holy Spirit

Lesson Starts

Text: Galatians 5:26, Luke 6:44

Memory Verse: “But the fruit of the Spirit is love, joy, peace, patience, kindness, goodness, faithfulness, 23 gentleness and self-control. Against such things there is no law.” - Galatians 5:22

Bottom Line : The fruit of the Spirit is outward evidence that the Holy Spirit lives inside the Christian.

Lesson Objective : In this lesson kids will recognize the various ways that a Christian grows to become more like Jesus once they have received the fullness of the Holy Spirit.

Lesson Introduction

A fruit is a product of the tree from which it is plucked, the tree is the product of the seed from which it springs. This statement underpins what we would be learning today.

Lesson Insight/Biblical Background

Paul the Apostle emphasized the importance of a life that is lived in the fullness of Christ through the influence of the Holy Spirit. If we live in the Spirit, we should also walk in the Spirit. Paul contrasted the life that is lived in the flesh (Gal.5:19-21) with the life lived in the Spirit. Paul’s point is that the more a believer yields to the Spirit, the less he succumbs to the acts of sinful nature—the flesh. The more she yields to the Spirit, the she bears the fruit of the Spirit.

Lesson Commentary

I. The Character of The Fruit

Jesus said, “No good tree bears bad fruit, nor does a bad tree bear good fruit. For each tree is known by its own fruit…” (Luke 6:43-44a). A fruit is a product of the tree from which it is plucked, the tree is the product of the seed from which it springs. Peter said, we are born again, not of corruptible seed, but of incorruptible, through the living and enduring word of God (1 Pet. 1:23). John wrote that no one who is born of God will continue to sin, because God's seed remains in them; they cannot go on sinning, because they have been born of God (1John 3:9). Paul in our text tags along same line of the allusion of Jesus, Peter and John to the imageries of the seed, tree and fruit, when he used the metaphor of the fruit to illustrate the disposition of a life that is filled or influenced by the Holy Spirit (vv.22-23). The fruit of the Spirit refers therefore to the character or nature of the person who is born of the Spirit and led by the Spirit. In other words, to have the fruit of the Spirit, is to have the character (nature, quality, disposition, personality) of the Holy Spirit. The fruit of the Spirit derives from the life of Christ. That is the life we received, when we got born again. The seed of Christ was planted in us at new birth. The Holy Spirit is the facilitating agent of the new birth experience (Titus 3:5). The fruit of the Spirit is therefore the fruit (character, nature) of Christ.

II. The Characteristics of The Fruit

Paul goes on to highlight the characteristics that depict the fruit of the Spirit. What it simply means is that anyone with the character of the Spirit, will manifest certain characteristics peculiar to the personality of the Holy Spirit, invariably the personality of Christ. This characteristics (nine in number) are virtues that are cultivated by the Holy Spirit in the one who is born of and led by the Spirit. After all, “…that that which is born of the Spirit is spirit” (John 3:6b). Let’s examine these virtues:

A. Love: The word used for love in the text depicts God's love and also of genuine Christian love, which reflects and acts in accordance with God's love (e.g.1 John 4:7-8).It has been described as an intense desire to please God and to do good to mankind. A Christian should show love toward others and seek their highest good, no matter who or what, just as God does (Matt. 5:43-48). 1 John 3:18 says we are to love in deed and in truth and not in word nor tongue. ‘Love’ is listed first because Paul rates it to be the most important of the virtues (1 Co 13:13) and because all of the others may be understood to derive from it.

B. Joy: Joy is a distinctive of a life that is born of the Spirit. True joy comes with the believer's salvation and assurance of an eternal destiny. It is the cheerful disposition of a regenerated soul. Like every other virtue in the list, joy is a quality and not a feeling. It is therefore not determined by surrounding situations—pleasant or unpleasant. The joy of the Lord is what givesthe believer the surge of strength in his daily walk with God (Neh. 8:10).

C. Peace: This is the calm, quiet, and order, which takes place in a regenerated soul. It does not change according to external circumstances. It goes beyond the absence of strife or war. Wholeness or completeness are the closest words to the Hebrew rendering of the word ‘peace’. Thus to know true peace is both to attain personal fulfillment and to enjoy full and healthy relationships with God and fellow human beings (Heb. 12:14; Rom. 12:18). In Philippians 4:7, Paul talks about the peace of God, which surpasses all understanding. It immune the believer’s heart and mind against anxiety and fear. It’s not something you can figure out, not even your psychologist can explain it.

D. Patience: It is translated long-suffering in some other Bible versions. That is actually the idea. It is a virtue that bears long. It bears with the frailties and provocations of others, from the consideration that God has borne long with ours. It also bears up also through all the troubles and difficulties of life without murmuring or languishing. It submits cheerfully to every dispensation of God's providence, and thus deriving benefit from every occurrence, having the understanding that that in all things God works for the good of those who love him, who have been called according to his purpose (Rom.8:28).

E. Kindness: Rendered as gentleness in some translations, it is a virtue that is an outgrowth of God’s kindness that leads people to repentance (Rom 2:4). Anyone who has benefited from God’s kind disposition is therefore positioned to show kindness to others. The quality of being kind includes being friendly, being benevolent, taking thought of others, sweetness of disposition, gentleness in dealing with others, affability, acts of hospitality, readiness to help others without strings attached, expressing gracious, serviceable, polite dispositions, the ability to act for the welfare of those getting on your nerves. It is not cranky or nasty.

F. Goodness: This virtue takes it root from God’s good nature. It is of the nature of God to be good. This characteristic is not changeable or diminishing. It find expression in the consistent desire, not only to abstain from every appearance of evil, but to be and do good to others to the utmost of one’s ability. Christians are admonished to hold fast to that which is good (Gal 6:21) and seek to do good things (1Thess. 5:15) for all people (Gal 6:9-10. They should not neglect opportunities to be of help (Heb. 13:16) or be weary of doing so 2Thess. 3:13). Indeed, they should be `rich in good deeds' (1Tim. 6:18) and maintain a good walk before the Lord (1Pet. 3:16).

G. Faithfulness: Sometimes, it is not clear whether this virtue is faith or faithfulness. It should however be understood that faithfulness must not be divorced from its relation to the believer's faith. Faith is expressed in an absolute commitment to the person and work of Christ. Indeed, true faith produces faithfulness. Thus faith and faithfulness are inevitably bound together, faithfulness being the natural outflow of genuine faith. The emphasis here refers to fidelity—which is a corollary punctuality in performing promises. It describes conscientious carefulness in preserving what is committed to one’s trust, in restoring it to its proper owner. It is being responsible and accountable in transacting the business one is entrusted with. It doesn’t betray the secret of a friend.

H. Meekness: It is rendered gentleness in some translations, and humility in others. Jesus spoke of Himself, “…I am gentle and humble in heart...” (Matt 11:29) Meekness is the attitude of the heart that flows towards others, with grace. It doesn’t seek reprisals of hurts. It is not brash in responding to insults. In fact it is not moved by insults. This is the virtue that describes the attitude of believers even when defending the faith; they are to courteously and respectfully give an answer to anyone who asks about the faith (1 Pet. 3:15-16). In all these instances meekness is not to be equated with weakness but rather it involves an inner God-given strength of character, which shields a person from arrogance.

I. Self-control: Temperance, as some translations put it, is the virtue that subdues the indulgent tendencies of one’s carnal faculties. It includes the dominion over all evil tendencies, and may denote continence, chastity, self-discipline, moderation in regard to all indulgences, including eating, drinking, sleeping and so on. The sense here is, that the influences of the Holy Spirit on the heart make a person moderate in all indulgences; instruct in restraining one’s passions, and to govern oneself; and to subdue all inordinate affection (Rom13:14; Titus2:2).

Discussion Questions/Activities

1. What new insights have you learnt from today’s lesson?

2. In which of characteristics are you deficient?

3. What do you intend to do to increase your fruitfulness?

Lesson Summary

  1. You may not know it, but when you became born again (born of the Spirit), the fruit of the Spirit was planted inside you in seed form. The characteristics begins manifest in your life as you submit to the leading of the Holy Spirit
  2. The fruit of the Spirit supersedes your inbred or acquired characteristics that may not be commendable. For example, you may not be a naturally patient person, but you respond to the Spirit, your character is imbued and empowered to produce patience.
  3. Your connectedness to Christ and obedience to the Spirit is vital to your continued fruitfulness.
  4. The more you manifest the characteristics of the fruit, the more you become like Christ. Christ-likeness is the ultimate goal of bearing the fruit of the Spirit.
