2017 Teens Sunday School Manual

Lesson 41

Praying with Boldness

Lesson Starts

Text: Acts 4:23-31

Memory Verse: “Now, Lord, consider their threats and enable your servants to speak your word with great boldness. 30 Stretch out your hand to heal and perform miraculous signs and wonders through the name of your holy servant Jesus." - Acts 4:29-30

Bottom Line: No achievement without courage and boldness.

Lesson Objective: This lesson will motivate teens to prayerfully deal with life challenges with courage and boldness

Lesson Introduction

Have you ever been scolded, punished or ridiculed for doing or wanting to do the right thing? Have you suffered any unfairness or disadvantage from people, just because you wouldn’t do the wrong thing or because you chose to be positively different? How did you feel? Terrible, unappreciated, wronged, mistreated, victimised, persecuted… it is possible words may not be sufficient to capture how you felt. Perhaps you would be able to relate with the ordeal of Peter and John in our text, when they were seized and locked away for being the nice guys.

Lesson Insight/Biblical Background

Peter and John were arrested and thrown into jail for preaching in the temple about Jesus Christ. This was after God had used them to heal the lame beggar at the beautiful gate. The crowd who saw the miracle happen had become Peter and John’s congregation and this didn’t go down well with the religious rulers. They didn’t want the apostles to preach to the people about Jesus, especially about the fact that he resurrected having been crucified. So, they got them locked up and tried to intimidate them. Peter and John, however, wouldn’t be intimidated, they boldly replied their captors: "Whether it's right in God's eyes to listen to you rather than to God, you decide. As for us, there's no question — we can't keep quiet about what we've seen and heard" (Acts 4:19-20 MSG). Since the religious authorities, couldn’t find any reason to punish the disciples, they had to let them go—but not without some more threats. However, it appeared the authorities’ threats didn’t do much in wearing out the courage of the apostles. Let’s find out why?

Lesson Outline

I. Courage in Company

II. Courage in Prayer

III. Courage in Purpose

IV. Courage in Power

Lesson Commentary

I. Courage in Company

After their release, Peter and John headed back to their friends and fellow believers to let them in on what had happened. It felt good to be in good company and to unburden their trouble. They were not alone in this. Being in the company of their friends, who shared their sufferings boosted their courage to hold on to their conviction. A friend in need is a friend indeed.

II. Courage in Prayer

It wasn’t just about the company. It was also that they knew the right thing to do when there was trouble. They prayed. They prayed together. They prayed aright. They didn’t go into a panicky frenzy at the threats of the authorities. They were motivated to pray by their trust in God. They acknowledged that God was greater than the difficulty they were faced with. They took confidence in the fact the God who created the heavens and the earth was their God. He can take charge of the challenge in their life. Their courage was bolstered by the word of God and by the testimonies of those who had walked the journey of faith before them.

III. Courage in Purpose

In their prayers, they weren’t bothered about self-preservation or the destruction of their enemies. They saw the threats of the authorities as being against God’s purpose for which they lived— the spread of the Gospel of Jesus Christ. This was the purpose that motivated them to pray. For this same cause, Herod, Pilate, the power of Rome and of the Jews put together had tried to stop Jesus, but they failed. In the face of threats, they prayed for boldness to be committed to the purpose of God for their lives— preaching the Gospel. No power could scare away the purpose of God in and for their lives. It wasn’t just about them, it was about purpose!

IV. Courage in Power

As they brought their prayers to a close, they prayed for the miraculous to happen in their ministry of the Word. This line of prayer is indicative in of their faith in the supernatural workings of God. As they participated in God’s purpose, they also wanted to experience God’s power being manifest through them. For sure, when the power of God becomes apparent in and through their lives, just as it happened in the case of Peter and John in the healing of the lame beggar, they couldn’t be bothered about the hostilities of the authorities.

Discussion Questions/Activities

1. What can we learn from the way the apostles responded to the threats in their lives?

2. What is the significance of building a strong peer-faith culture in facing life challenges?

3. Can you identify the connection between God’s purpose for your life and the challenges or threats you are facing?

Lesson Summary

The fact that we are identified as children of God and followers of Christ, targets us for challenges that would want to shake the very core of our conviction. However, in the place of prayer (not motivated by fear, vengefulness, or despair but by absolute trust in God), and in the company of our God-given friends, we must take courage in the purpose for which we have been called and the power of God to back this purpose and to preserve us in Christ for the hope of our calling (Read Heb. 3:6).
