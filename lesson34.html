2017 Teens Sunday School Manual

Lesson 34

Saved By Grace

Lesson Starts

Text: Acts 15:1-12

Memory Verse: “For it is by grace you have been saved, through faith — and this not from yourselves, it is the gift of God— 9 not by works, so that no one can boast.” - Ephesians 2:8, 9

Bottom Line: We can only be saved by grace alone.

Lesson Objective: At the end of this lesson, teens should

understand that works and activities do not save but we can only be saved by grace.

Lesson Introduction

The love of God for humanity resulted in the redemptive act and processes by Jesus Christ paying the wages of our sin on the cross of Calvary. By His precious blood He made atonement for our sin and purchased for us an eternal inheritance. We now have free access to boldly enter God’s throne room (Heb. 4:16). We are saved by grace through faith. Grace is defined as unmerited favour: A free gift of God’s love. Faith is believing and trusting from the heart. That means, if we can believe God and His redemptive plan through Jesus, we have access to His free gift of grace, “But as many as received him, to them gave he power to become the sons of God, even to them that believe on his name” (John 1:12).

Lesson Insight/Biblical Background

Some men came down from Judea to Antioch and insisted that circumcision was essential for justification. Perhaps they based their theology on such passages as Gen. 17:14 and Exo. 12:48-49. However, they were sure to cause a severe division in the church, so their teaching brought Paul and Barnabas into sharp dispute and debate with them. The church at Antioch felt it was wise to discuss the matter with the apostles and elders in Jerusalem. So they commissioned Paul and Barnabas for the task and wisely sent some other believers along as witnesses. These witnesses would protect Paul and Barnabas against being accused of distorting the facts.

Lesson Outline

I. Contention with Gentile Believers

II. Church Elders Rule on Gentile Believers

III. Salvation is for Everyone Who Believes

Lesson Commentary

I. Contention with Gentile Believers

The fanatical Jews must have imagined that for a non-Jew to become a worshipper of God, he must first convert to Judaism. They must have imagined that to be a Christian, one had to first become a Jew, or at least surrender one’s own culture and identity, and adopt the lifestyle of an Israelite. These Jewish believers travelled to the Gentile churches, teaching that, "Except ye be circumcised after the manner of Moses, ye cannot be saved (Acts 15:1). There was serious debate between these Jewish believers with Paul and Barnabas. The believers in Antioch did not agree with these Jewish teachers believing they were not sent by the apostles, and their teaching did not represent the official position of the Jerusalem congregation. They decided to send a delegation, led by Paul and Barnabas, to bring this issue to the apostles at Jerusalem.

II. Church Elders Rule on Gentile Believers

The apostles and elders at Jerusalem met to consider this question with many other believers in attendance. The problem was no small one. The presentation of Apostle Peter channelled a good course for balanced judgment with his submission, “Now therefore why tempt ye God, to put a yoke upon the neck of the disciples, which neither our fathers nor we were able to bear?” (Acts 15:10). After much discussion, Apostle James gave the verdict, “Wherefore my sentence is, that we trouble not them, which from among the Gentiles are turned to God: But that we write unto them, that they abstain from pollutions of idols, and from fornication, and from things strangled, and from blood” (Acts 15:19-20).

III. Salvation is for Everyone Who Believes

Our memory verse, Ephesians 2:8-9, gives a clear picture of the will and mind of God concerning salvation. It is by grace through faith. Romans chapter 10:8-10 explains how we can be saved. Romans 10:12-13 gives the conclusion, “For there is no difference between the Jew and the Greek: for the same Lord over all is rich unto all that call upon him. For whosoever shall call upon the name of the Lord shall be saved.”

Discussion Questions/Activities

1. What was the contention of the fanatical Jews?

2. What do you understand by the concept “Saved by Grace”?

3. What are some church traditions that undermine the concept of “Saved by Grace” today?

Lesson Summary

1. Some Jewish believers contended that for a non-Jew to become a worshipper of God, he must first convert to Judaism. They believed that to be a Christian, one has to first become a Jew, or at least surrender one’s own culture and identity, and adopt the lifestyle of an Israelite. They argued that Gentile Christians should be circumcised (Acts 15:1).

2. There are so many such conditions we witness in the body of Christ today. Some strange doctrines have been introduced. That is why the scripture warns us, “Be not carried about with divers and strange doctrines. For it is a good thing that the heart be established with grace; not with meats, which have not profited them that have been occupied therein” (Heb. 13:9).

3. We must watch out against teachings that emphasize forms and traditions, self-help tips and human-efforts over the truth of God’s word about our salvation. Let’s not be like the Galatians, who having received the Gospel by the Spirit, allowed themselves to be swept of by the dogma of rules taught by men(Gal. 3:1-3).
