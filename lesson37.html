2017 Teens Sunday School Manual

Lesson 37

A Choice to Be Just

Lesson Starts

Text: Ezra 7:1, 6, 21-28; Jeremiah 7:1-15

Memory Verse : “For Ezra had devoted himself to the study and observance of the Law of the Lord, and to teaching its decrees and laws in Israel.” - Ezra 7:10

Bottom Line : The choice to please is God is the choice to be just.

Lesson Objective : This lesson will help the teens to prepare in making the right choices for God.

Lesson Introduction

Lesson Introduction

J.K Rowling said, “It is our choices that show what we truly are, far more than our abilities.” One distinguishing factor between human and other creatures is the “will power”, ability to make choices. Life is all about making choices. All of life is defined by choice. In every generation, man struggles between two major choices; standing for God or taking a stand for the devil. Standing for God is a noble course. God wants His people to willingly choose Him.

Lesson Insight/Biblical Background

In spite of God’s love for Israel, their sins still led them into captivity in Babylon. Israel had gone astray from the path that Yahweh had charted for them. Before the visitation of judgement, God sent Jeremiah to warn them to desist from their evil doing. God gave them opportunity for repentance, but they would not yield. They chose to displease God. They were conceited. They had false hope and false confidence, created in their hearts by their leaders.

Lesson Outline

I. The Conceit of An Unjust Choice

II. The Consequence of An Unjust Choice

III. The Conscience of A Just Choice

Lesson Commentary

I. The Conceit of An Unjust Choice

First, the people placed their confidence in the temple buildings. They were under the impression that the mere fact that the temple was located in Jerusalem would spare the city from captivity. They were wrong. God told them through Jeremiah that it would take genuine repentance to reverse the situation (Jer. 7:1-7). Repentance would mean not only true-hearted worship of the Lord, but also a change in how the people treated one another; that their dealings were to be just and honorable and without oppression. Second, they glossed over their obvious sins (Jer. 7:8-11). They were deluded into thinking that they could sin and go scot-free. After committing sin, they would go to the temple thinking that God would absolve them. That again was a fatal error, because God wasn’t going to spear them, no matter how they may try to patronize him. They are reminded of what happened in Shiloh, to their ancestors (Jer. 7:12). Shiloh is where the tabernacle had stood before the temple had been built at Jerusalem, and was at that time the center of the worship to Yahweh. The Ark of the Covenant was there.

II. The Consequence of An Unjust Choice

It was at Shiloh that the Ark of the Covenant had been captured by the Philistines and the unfaithful people of Israel defeated (1 Sam. 4:10-11). They too had been mistaken to think that the physical presence of the ark would immune them from defeat. They were not faithful to the Lord in their conduct and service, they had a false confidence in the ark to give them victory in battle against the Philistines. Jeremiah warns that the fate of Jerusalem would be like that of Shiloh. Just as the ark did not save their ancestors from the onslaught of the Philistines, rather the ark was even captured, likewise, the temple will not save them from the captivity of the Babylonians, and even the temple will come to ruins, except they repent. And that was exactly what happened, because they failed to heed the warning of the prophet and turn from their wicked ways. For effectively 70 years Israel was in captivity as Jeremiah had prophesied (Jer. 25:8-12; 29:10). Their cities were devastated, their temple destroyed, their fortunes diminished, their honor denigrated and their children deprived.

III. The Conscience of a Just Choice

Cyrus the Great issued the decree for the release and return of the Jews, the repentant remnant of the people of God, to rebuild their devastated cities and the temple in Jerusalem (Ezra 1:1-4). The Jews began their return in batches. About 20 years after, Ezra led another batch of exiles to Jerusalem. Ezra had been born in captivity, but grew up to know God. He had the privilege to continue to work in the service of the king – Artaxerxes, but He chose to take a stand for a Godly cause. Ezra was a priest by birth, but his pedigree was not the basis for his devotion to God. There were others who were from the priestly lineage in his days, but Ezra chose to make a difference. Ezra was an effective servant of God because he had prepared himself to do God’s work. He was instrumental to restoring the people to the proper worship of Yahweh. First, he became a role model to the people, in his personal observance and obedience to God’s laws. Second, he challenged the people to take a persistent stand for God who has been faithful enough to bring them back with favor from exile. In Ezra’s day many of the people had broken God’s law and had married pagans. Ezra demanded that the people come out of these unscriptural marriages (Ezra, chapters 9 and 10). Ezra also organized the priesthood according to the teaching of the Scriptures.

Discussion Questions/Activities

1. Read Hosea 6:6. What is the significance this text to Israel’s false confidence in the temple buildings and their religious practices?

2. It is often said that “history will repeat itself”. Should this have happened in the case of Israel, even after they were reminded of the error of Shiloh? (Justify your answer)

3. Learning from Ezra, what choices should we be making, in order not to repeat errors of the past?

Lesson Summary

In Ezra 7:10, we read: “For Ezra had set his heart to seek the law of Jehovah, and to do it, and to teach in Israel statutes and ordinances.” Unlike his ancestors who made an unjust choice in disobeying God’s principles, Ezra chose to make a difference in his generation. For him, the knowledge of God was a matter of the heart. His choice was motivated by his love for God and God’s people. Ezra came back from captivity expecting to find the people serving the Lord, but to his dismay he found the opposite. He chose to turn the tide of disobedience in the people. He mobilized the people to a spiritual reawakening and reshaped the community in accordance with the scriptures. Ezra’s life is a model for us in making a righteous choice in pleasing God in our lives.
