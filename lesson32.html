2017 Teens Sunday School Manual

Lesson 32

Peter Defends His Actions

Lesson Starts

Text: Acts 11:1-18

Memory Verse: “Then I heard a voice telling me, 'Get up, Peter. Kill and eat.' "I replied, 'Surely not, Lord! Nothing impure or unclean has ever entered my mouth.' "The voice spoke from heaven a second time, 'Do not call anything impure that God has made clean.' - Acts 11:7-9

Bottom Line: Our lifestyle and relationship with other believers of other persuasion or denomination must be guided by the truth of God’s word and the direction of the Spirit and not by the bias of church tradition.

Lesson Objective: This lesson will help the teens to learn

Cultivate a more open heart in relating with other believers beyond denominational boundaries and bias.

Lesson Introduction

The major event that has characterized the preaching of the Gospel is persecution. This however should not pose a limitation or fear in us because persecution has proved to be a means of advancing the course of the Gospel, strengthening believers’ faith and bringing glory to God. Apostle James admonishes us, “My brethren, count it all joy when ye fall into divers temptations; knowing this, that the trying of your faith worketh patience. But let patience have her perfect work, that ye may be perfect and entire, wanting nothing” (James 1:2-4). Apostle Paul encouraged Timothy, “Yes, and all who desire to live godly in Christ Jesus will suffer persecution” (Tim. 3:12-13). The Master says, “These things I have spoken to you, that in Me you may have peace. In the world you will have tribulation; but be of good cheer, I have overcome the world" (John 16:33). This is the needed encouragement for us.

Lesson Insight/Biblical Background

When Apostle Peter returned to Jerusalem he was met by members of the strong legalistic party in the church of Judea, commonly referred to as "the circumcision", who rebuked him for having fellowship with Gentiles and eating with them. It seems these Jewish believers did not yet understand the relationship between law and grace, Jews and Gentiles, and Israel and the church. Most of the ordinary Jewish believers were having a difficult time understanding the transition (Acts 21:20). It was not only a matter of religion, but also of culture; and cultural habits are very hard to break. We must avoid zeal without knowledge. It is good to be zealous, but we must add adequate knowledge to be effective and productive in our Christian service.

Lesson Outline

I. The Contention with Peter (Acts 11:1-3)

II. The Defence of Peter (ACTS 11:4-17 )

III. Knowing The Grace of God (Acts 11:18)

Lesson Commentary

I. The Contention with Peter

Both the apostles and the brethren in Judea contended with Apostle Peter for going to the Gentiles. The expression ‘circumcised believers’ describes Jewish Christians who still held to the Law of Moses. They transfer their idea of Judaism into Christianity (Acts 10:45; 15:5; 21:20; Gal 2:12). The accusation lodged against Peter was that he went into the house of uncircumcised men and ate with them. I think they were more concerned with Peter eating with them (cf. Mark 2:16; Luke 15:2; Gal 2:12). This gives even greater significance to Peter's vision (Acts 10:9-16). Eating with someone was a mark of acceptance and fellowship (cf. 1 Cor. 5:11). This problem could have caused a serious break in the church if God had not given Peter a vision ahead of time.

II. The Defence of Peter

Peter narrated the circumstances that led to what had occurred, including his vision (Acts 11:5-7), his response to it (Acts 11:8-10), and the trip to Cornelius' house (Acts 11:11-14). In recounting what happened next, Peter made an important identification of the day of Pentecost with the Lord's prediction of the Holy Spirit baptism (Acts 1:4-5). Peter here pointedly said so by the phrase at the beginning, "just as we have," (Acts 10:47) and, "the same gift as He gave us” (Acts 11:17). Referring to what happened on the day of Pentecost.

III. Knowing The Grace of God

Peter's defense did not rest on what he himself did, but on what God did. If God had not made a distinction between Jews and Gentiles, why should Peter? The believers in Judea, Jerusalem then recognized that the conversion of Gentiles was initiated by God and that they should not stand in His way. This response had two significant results. First, it preserved the unity of the body of Christ, the church. Second, it bridged the gap between the Jews and the Gentiles. It clearly shows that Jesus died for all men (humanity). We are all saved by grace (Eph. 2:4-10).

Discussion Questions/Activities

1. The apostles and brethren in Judea contended with Peter for fellowshipping and eating with the Gentiles. If you were in Peter’s place what would be your reaction?

2. Why did they contend with Peter?

3. Analyze and explain the defence of Peter and the response of the people.

Lesson Summary

1. Peter had to contend with legalists in the church who chided him for going into the house of uncircumcised men and eating with them.

2. This kind of segregation and adversarial attitude possess great danger to the spread of the Gospel.

3. There are legalists today in the church. They always exhibit certain characteristics:

a. They become more, contentious, and judgmental towards others who don't do as they do.

b. They lack patience with others who are still growing in faith.

c. They usually like to control others to think and do as they do.

d. They have prideful and condescending attitudes toward other denominations who have different persuasions.

4. If you find yourself manifesting any of these attitudes, you may be a legalist and you need change of attitude.
