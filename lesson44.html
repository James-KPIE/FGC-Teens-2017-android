2017 Teens Sunday School Manual

Lesson 44

Serving Neighbours, Serving God

Lesson Starts

Text: Luke 10:25-37

Memory Verse: “So in everything, do to others what you would have them do to you, for this sums up the Law and the Prophets.” - Matthew 7:12

Bottom Line: Our love must be exhibited vertically and

horizontally.

Lesson Objective: At the end of this lesson, teens should be able to help their neighbours as they serve God.

Lesson Introduction

Serving neighbours, serving God—the question whether one relates with the other comes to mind, shouldn’t they be mutually exclusive, is one more important than the other, who is my neighbour? In today’s lesson, Jesus clarifies these questions, in response to a certain lawyer or scribe, who actually came to tease Jesus but ended up being the victim of his own mischief.

Lesson Insight/Biblical Background

The lawyer in the story was an expert in the Jewish law, (not a barrister at law) who knew and understood the details of the Jewish religion and traditions. He was more or less an Old Testament scholar, otherwise called a scribe. His speciality was in the Torah— the five books of Moses. The scribe came to ask Jesus a question, “what must I do to inherit eternal life” (v.25)? It was a good question asked with a bad motive, because the lawyer’s original intent was test Jesus. He wasn’t really seeking to be taught by Jesus, nor was he interested in finding the way to eternal life. He was only out to fault Jesus’ views about matters of the law. Jesus replied by referring His questioner back to the law. After all, as teacher of the law, he should know better. As expected, the scribe gave the right answer, quoting from Deuteronomy 6:5 and Leviticus 19:18. Jesus, acknowledge the correctness of his answer, and told him, “Do this and you will live” (v. 28). That didn’t seems to go down with the lawyer well, he gets defensive, especially about the “Love your neighbour as yourself” (v.27) part of the answer. Feeling obligated to justify himself, he asks Jesus a second question, “And who is my neighbour?” (v.29) Jesus answered him with the story of the Good Samaritan (vv. 30-35).

Lesson Commentary

Who is my neighbour? Anyone, you can be of help to anywhere is your neighbour.

I. The Example of Good Neighbourliness

The hero in Jesus’ story was the most unlikely person— the Samaritan. This wouldn’t sound so good in the ears of the lawyer who was a Jew and belonged to the elite class like the priest and the Levite in the story. Samaritans were disliked and disparaged by Jews. They were considered as outcast, unholy and without class. But in this narrative the priest and the Levite, who were actually the most esteemed stock of the Jewish religion, as a matter of status and profession, didn’t live up to expectation. They were supposed to be custodians of the same law about loving one’s neighbour, which their lawyer kin claimed to know, but as it turned out they goofed. They walked past a human being, mugged badly hurt and desperately needing help and left him for the dead. When the Samaritan, who actually on journey got to the crime scene, he stopped everything for the sake of the wounded stranger. It didn’t matter to him if he was going to miss his appointment. It didn’t matter whether the guy was a Jew, Samaritan or gentile. This was a fellow human being, who needed help, and as long as it was within his capacity, the Samaritan was willing to help. He went all the way to ensure the care and the recovery of the injured stranger. In many ways, the Samaritan was different from the two religious leaders, but one thing that especially distinguishes the Samaritan from the Jews, was compassion. When the two Jewish religious leaders saw the injured man, they seem to be repulsed, just ignored and avoided him. The Samaritan, on the other hand moved with compassion, did everything possible to minister to the needs of the injured victim. The Samaritan sacrificed his time and money to help this stranger. Compassion, as used in our text draws on the Hebrew concept faithfulness between individuals that results in human kindness, mercy, and pity. It is an outgrowth of godly piety as underscored in Micah 6:8: "He has showed you, O man, what is good. And what does the Lord require of you? To act justly and to love mercy, and to walk humbly with your God.” Now Jesus drives home His point. He asks the lawyer which of the three proved to be a neighbour to the wounded man, and the lawyer, now feeling cornered, was forced to reply, “The one who had mercy on him” (v.37). To the distaste of the lawyer, the Samaritan gets the award of good neighbourliness, and to add insult to injury, he gets to announce the award.

II. The Ethic of Godly Neighbourliness

Jesus admonishes the lawyer, “go and do likewise” in consistence with the earlier admonition following the lawyer's answer to the first question, “do this and you will live.” (v.28). For Jesus it wasn’t enough just to define what neighbour means or to argue about and around the technicalities of neighbourliness. He commands the lawyer to follow the Samaritan’s example of neighbourliness, to show mercy to a fellow human being who is in need. Neighbourliness was a big issue for the Jews because of the characteristic exclusiveness of the people. They were self-consciousness of being the ‘chosen ones’ sealed in the rite of circumcision. They had a superiority complex, in Nigerian parlance what would be referred to as the “ I better pass my neighbour” mentality. This complex of being the ‘elected ones’ made them particularly loyal to their own kind and, at the same time, permitted the tendency to be prejudiced and even hostile to non-Israelites. It is therefore not surprising that the priest and Levite failed the test of neighbourliness in our text. This was in spite of the legislations Moses gave them to encourage compassion and justice for the non-Jew, For example, the prohibition of harvesting crops entirely; instead, some crops were to be left for the poor and the alien ( Lev. 19:9-10 ); and every third year a tithe was to be shared with the sojourners ( Deut. 14:29 ; 26:12-13 ). Essentially, the Mosaic laws demand both compassion and justice to be guaranteed for the foreigner because God loves the sojourner (Deut. 10:18). But the traditions of the people overtime became contrary to what was supposed to be the norm. To Jesus, the story of the Good Samaritan illustrates how the two most important commandments in Deuteronomy 6: 4–5 (“you shall love the Lord your God…”) and Leviticus 19: 18 (“you shall love your neighbour as yourself”), are intimately connected. The love of neighbour springs from God’s love for creation. The commitment to others, therefore, is an expression of the faith in God and commitment to God, as God commits to human beings in His creation.

Discussion Questions/Activities

1. How do you understand the concept of neighbourliness from today’s lesson?

2. How is it applicable in our everyday living?

3. What tendencies of “I better pass my neighbour” have you noticed with church people, how do such attitude negate good neighbourliness?

Lesson Summary

1. Our memory verse (Matthew 7:12) captures one of the most profound statements that the Jesus ever made. “Therefore, whatever you want men to do to you, do also to them, for this is the law and the prophets.” It is often called the ‘golden rule’ of life. The golden rule does not teach that we are to do to others what we would like them to do to us in order that they will do it to us. For example, you buy your friend lunch, so he or she will by you lunch some other day. Jesus speaks of an unconditional love. When we do good to others, we expect nothing in return. A better way to interpret the rule is that we are to do to others as we would like to have done to ourselves if we were placed in their condition. If you do that, you can never go wrong. This resonates with the principle of neighbourliness, “Love your neighbour as yourself.”

2. Neighbourliness is understood as a response to the challenge of meeting human needs, indiscriminately. Being or becoming a neighbour, therefore, to Christians means taking responsibility for others out of compassion—not the easy, emotional compassion, but the tough, demanding compassion that answers the call to commitment, whether or not the object of our compassion is deserving.

3. This way we share the God kind of love with others, serving God in our neighbourly response to humanity.
