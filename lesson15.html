2017 Teens Sunday School Manual

Lesson 15

Christ Rides into Jerusalem (Palm Sunday)

Lesson Starts

Text: Matthew 21:1-11; Luke19:11-40

Memory Verse: “Blessed is he who comes in the name of the Lord.

From the house of the Lord we bless you.” - Psalm 118:26

Bottom Line: Palm Sunday, otherwise known in the Bible as the Triumphal entry of Jesus Christ into Jerusalem Is the beginning Of His passion week.

Lesson Objective: At the end of the lesson, the teens will be able to understand the purpose and meaning of Christ’s Kingship.

Lesson Introduction

Christ’s triumphal entry into Jerusalem marked the beginning of what is known to Christians as the ‘Passion Week’ – the week of His suffering, crucifixion and death for mankind. It was a precarious week for Jesus and his disciples. Everything happened so fast. What started out to be a pomp at the beginning of the week, dimmed into a weekend of a chain of inauspicious episodes that was to redefine the lives of the disciples, the history of the Jews and ultimately the destiny of humanity.

Lesson Insight/Biblical Background

It was the Passover week. Pilgrims flowed into Jerusalem from outlying towns and villages and from other parts of the known world for the celebration of the Passover feast. The feast was commemorative of the deliverance of the children of Israel out of Egypt. The Passover celebration re-enacted the story of freedom after more than four hundred years of Egyptian bondage. The recurring allusions to that deliverance in the Old Testament indicate that it was a source of hope for the nation's future redemption. Some Bible scholars suggest that close to 2 million people come into Jerusalem during the celebration. The city went agog with commercial activities. It was usually a time of buying and selling, as pilgrims brought in their wares, not only of such domestic goods as clothing, jewellery, and exotic ointments, but also of spices, herbs, condiments, wheat, fish, and wine used throughout the week-long festivities. Of utmost importance to pilgrims, however, was the buying of sheep and goats for sacrifice at the temple. This was the mood of the city when Jesus rode in.

Lesson Commentary

I. The Regal Entry of An Unlikely King

As Jesus rode on a borrowed donkey from Bethphage on the Mount of Olives down into the Kidron valley, east into Jerusalem, excited pilgrims, especially from Galilee, cut palm branches and spread their clothes on the ground for him to ride on in a red carpet style. They shouted at the top of their voices: “Hosanna to the Son of David! Blessed is he who comes in the name of the Lord! Hosanna in the highest!” (v. 9) By this time, the whole city was in an uproar, asking “who can this be?” The cheering crowd answered, “This is the prophet Jesus from Nazareth of Galilee.” (v.11) Jesus Christ well was known to the Galileans, but not too known to the Judean crowd. Until now Jesus had rather been quiet about his public identity. On this occasion, he chose to make his entry conspicuous to fulfil the prophecy of the coming Messiah in Zechariah 9:9-10. Apparently, the disciples and the Galilean pilgrims knew about the prophecy, hence their acclamation “Son of David!” indicating that they recognized Jesus' ancestry and were hopeful that he was the long awaited Messiah.

II. The Unusual Reign of An Awaited King

Whether the people were certain or not of Jesus’ messianic identity, they were very much on point to have proclaimed him King. For Christ was indeed the promised Messiah-King and not just the King of Israel, as the crowd had presumed, but the King of all. What kind of king rides on a lowly donkey and without his legion or at least a cohort? Indeed the kings of the earth would rather ride on horses and chariots accompanied by a large intimidating force, but Jesus was unlike any earthly king. His kingdom is not of this world (John 18:36). While earthly kings are pompous, ruthless and oppressed their subjects, unleashing war, terror and destruction in their wake, Jesus came as a gentle King, offering peace, salvation and liberty to humanity. Earthly kings conquer territories and forcefully impose their rule over vassal domains, but Christ conquer the hearts of people with His love. Secular kings surround themselves with fierce army of soldiers, distancing themselves from people in a demi-god style, Christ would rather identify with everyday people, demonstrating His connection with their humanity and plight. His kingship is marked service to humanity and especially to those who choose to come under His reign. He would care for them as their Shepherd.

Lesson Summary

Although Jesus’ kingship is not in the same manner as earthly kings, His reign does have an impact on this world. Wherever there are people who have trusted Him and yielded to His reign, He is King. One day He shall return and establish a righteous kingdom on earth. Those who have subscribed to His rule by making Him their Lord and Savior, will participate fully in the reign of His kingdom, but those who reject Him now, will have themselves to blame as they would have to face judgement. Presently, all those who have accepted Jesus as their Lord and King, and are working out the ethics of His kingdom by their faith and works are already participating in the Kingdom.

Discussion Questions/Activities

1. Why did Jesus have to ride into Jerusalem, rather than walk, as His usual practice as He went from place to place?

2. What significance does Jesus’ style of kingship hold for those who want to be part of His kingdom?

3. How should the events of Christ’s Passion Week, death and resurrection affect us as Christian teenagers?
