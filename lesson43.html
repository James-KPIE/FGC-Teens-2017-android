2017 Teens Sunday School Manual

Lesson 43

Speaking Up for God

Lesson Starts

Text: Acts 5:27-29, 33-42

Memory Verse: “Peter and the other apostles replied: "We must obey God rather than men!” - Acts 5:29

Bottom Line: Fear God and respect persons

Lesson Objective : At the end of this lesson, teens should be able to share the gospel with others without fear or intimidation.

Lesson Introduction

Every President has a spokesperson saddled with responsibility of representing the President’s views on such a vast range of complicated issues before a hostile press and the public in general. When Goodluck Jonathan was president, it was Rueben Abati, who was his spokesman, and now for President Muhammadu Buhari, it is Femi Adesina. When these men stand before the press or public, they couldn’t care less about who they are facing. They speak with presidential confidence and candour. They air the opinion of the one they represent like it is their own. The press may be cynical, the public may be incredulous, but that isn’t enough to deter the conviction of these men about who they are representing. In the same manner, the apostles were God’s spokesmen as they presented the Gospel before the Jews and gentiles.

Lesson Insight/Biblical Background

After the incident of Ananias and Sapphira, there was increased fear of God and humility in the people. More miracles and healing took place and many more people believed the Gospel. The Church became larger still, as crowds from suburbs and surrounding towns flowed into Jerusalem to have their sick and tormented healed by the apostles. This had become too much for the religious leaders, to be precise the Sadducees, who were uncomfortably jealous of the popularity of the apostles. The high priests and his associates moved to arrest and jail the apostles, but the angel of God, released them miraculously. The next morning when the full council of the Jewish elders gathered for a trial of the apostles, they couldn’t find them in jail, even though the prisons locks were intact. There hadn’t been any jail break and the guards had not been disposed of their weapons. To their dismay, they found the apostles teaching the people at the temple courts, as the angel had instructed them.

Lesson Outline

I. Speaking with Conviction

II. Speaking from Commitment

Lesson Commentary

I. Speaking with Conviction

The apostles were summoned before the council, although quietly, in order avoid a tumult from the crowd who were already rooting for the apostles. The Sanhedrin didn’t really have anything new to say, except the repetition of their earlier ruling (Acts 4:19-20) that the apostles should stop preaching Jesus’ name, claiming that their teaching was indicting the Jewish leaders of being guilty of the death of Jesus. The Sanhedrin’s intimidation tactics wasn’t working at all. They didn’t even get an apology from Peter, who spoke up in the same manner he had done before: “We must obey God rather than men (5:29)!” If the religious leaders had an exaggerated idea of their own importance and power, the apostles were not impressed. These were mere men, and the apostles were committed to obeying God. The leaders would have been astonished and abashed at the audacity of the apostles, having known that they were mere commoners. What changed? It is called conviction. The apostles were speaking and acting with conviction.

A. Nature of Conviction

Dictionaries usually define conviction as a fixed or strong belief. Biblical conviction is deeper than just a strong belief. Having convictions is being so thoroughly convinced that something is absolutely true that you take a stand for it regardless of the consequences. Howards Hendricks puts it this way: “A belief is something you will argue about. A conviction is something you will die for!” For the disciples the Gospel was something worth dying for, it wasn’t just a matter of identity that they were followers of Christ. Peter, who speaking here, was the same guy, who after he had denied Jesus thrice, went fishing. That was then. This Peter was different. What were the bases of the apostles’ conviction?

B. Conviction based on relationship with Christ

First, it was their relationship with Christ. Christ was the object of their conviction— not fame, not power. Even the religious leaders acknowledged that they had been with Jesus (4:13).

C. Conviction based on God’s Word

Second, their conviction was rooted in the truth of God’s word— in scriptures and in the authority and validity of what Christ had personally spoken to them. The grounding of their conviction in the scriptures could be recognized, from Peter’s speech and the disciples’ prayer prior to the enrolment of Matthias (1:16-25), Peter’s sermon at Pentecost (2:14b-40b), Peter’s speech in Solomon’s portico of the Temple, after the healing of the beggar at the beautiful gate (3:12-26), Peter’s speech to the Jewish authorities after his and John’s arrest (4:8b-12, 19b-20), the prayer of the apostles and their friends, which led to a fresh revival (4:24b-30), and the speech of Peter and the apostles to the council, at their second arrest (5:29b-32). Their conviction was also based on all that Jesus said to them personally as recorded in the Gospels and in Acts—about the Holy Spirit (1:5:6), about their empowerment for their mission (1:7-8). It would be observed how much the apostles relied on and referred to scriptures (as at then the Old Testament Scriptures) as the basis of their thoughts and actions.

D. Conviction based on the effect of the Word

Peter told the Jewish council, “We are witnesses of these things…” (5:32), meaning that they were not just testimonies (whose thinking, words and lives affirmed) to the life and teachings of Christ, but they were also martyrs as people who had given up their lives for Christ. In other words, they were dead to themselves. The sense in which the word is used is captured in Paul’s words, “My old self has been crucified with Christ. It is no longer I who live, but Christ lives in me…” (Gal.2:20). This was made possible because the liberating power of the Gospel, which had not only transformed the apostles but the many thousands who believed.

E. Conviction based the Spirit’s empowerment and control

From the Pentecost on, the Holy Spirit empowered the apostles for great exploits that reinforced their conviction about their faith. The Miracles and healings wrought by the hands of the apostles through the power of the Spirit was a faith booster. Their conviction was also rooted in a Spirit-filled life, which exuded the character of Christ. Their love for one another, their faithfulness to the word of God and the ministry, their patience in the face of persecution, their kindness to their neighbours and enemies and every other virtue of the Spirit they exhibited were a proof of their faith and validated their message to the world, as God’s spokespersons.

II. Speaking from Commitment

Conviction births commitment. For the apostles conviction meant commitment. Commitment is the act of binding yourself (intellectually or emotionally) to a course of action. Commitment comes with the courage to act on one’s conviction in faith. The apostles would rather obey God even at the risk of being executed, as that would have been their fate (v. 33), had it not been for the cautionary intervention of Gamaliel, one of the Jewish leaders, a Pharisee (vv. 34-35, 40). Having been flogged and sternly warned to desist from proclaiming Christ, the apostles departed with joy from the presence of the Sanhedrin, because they were counted worthy to be dishonoured on account of the name of Christ. In the face of persecution, pain, shame, disgrace, and various threatened deaths, they did not cease from preaching and teaching the Gospel be it in private or public places. Instead of their sufferings whittling down their commitment to the cause of the Gospel, it further served as an impetuous (41-42) in advancing it.

Discussion Questions/Activities

1. Having learnt about what conviction is, would you say that your faith is based on conviction or cognition (knowledge), and why?

2. What are the likely factors that would dissuade you from boldly sharing your faith with your neighbours or school mates?

3. What decisions would you want to take about these deterring factors?

Lesson Summary

1. Our faith commitments must go beyond cognition (knowledge) to conviction, if we are go to be God’s spokesperson.

2. To speak up for God in an increasingly skeptical world, we need to build our relationship, with the Holy Spirit, through obedience to God’s word.

3. To cultivate a life obedience, we must be diligent in the study and meditation of God’s word (Josh. 1:8, 2 Tim. 2:15).
